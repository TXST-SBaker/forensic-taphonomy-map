About This Resource & How to Use

This self-contained website aggregates publicly available information on all known active human forensic taphonomy research facilities worldwide (as of April 2026). These outdoor laboratories enable critical studies on postmortem processes across climates, supporting PMI estimation, victim identification, law enforcement training, and disaster response.

Key Data Included
  • Facility name, university/entity, location & precise coordinates
  • Establishment date, current + historical directors
  • Body donation program links & contact info
  • Donor metrics/notes, published research highlights
  • Size, climate/environment, unique features
Quick Tips
  • Click any marker to see facility details
  • Use the search bar to find specific facilities
  • Filter by region using the buttons above the map
  • Click "Compare" to see 2-3 facilities side-by-side
Important: All facilities are secure, fenced, and closed to the public. Visits by appointment only for qualified researchers/LE. Always respect donor privacy, ethics guidelines, and facility rules. Never attempt unauthorized access.

🌡️ Regional Climate Overview

Average conditions for each region where facilities are located. These climates directly affect decomposition rates studied at the sites.

● International
Australia (AFTER): Temperate-subtropical, 15–28°C, 600–1200mm rain, wildfire risk
Canada (SSRT): Continental cold, −15 to 25°C, heavy snow, freeze-thaw cycles
Netherlands (ARISTA): Maritime temperate, 2–22°C, 700–900mm rain, mild winters
● US Northeast / Mid-Atlantic
Four distinct seasons, −10 to 30°C, 1000–1200mm precipitation, mixed forest/urban interface. Key for winter decomposition studies.
● US Southeast
Humid subtropical, 5–32°C, 1200–1600mm rain, high insect activity, rapid vegetation growth. Critical for understanding fast decomposition.
● Texas
Hot semi-arid to humid subtropical, 5–38°C, 300–1200mm rain (varies east-west), intense vulture activity, high desiccation rates in west.
● US West / Mountain
High-desert semi-arid, −10 to 35°C, low rainfall (~220mm), high altitude, extreme temperature swings, excellent for mummification/desiccation studies.
● Key Insight
These contrasting climates (cold boreal → hot arid → humid subtropical) allow researchers to develop region-specific postmortem interval (PMI) models that improve forensic accuracy worldwide.

Texas: +2.1°C, increased drought frequency → higher desiccation rates
Canada: −3.5 days of snow cover per decade → changing winter decomposition patterns
